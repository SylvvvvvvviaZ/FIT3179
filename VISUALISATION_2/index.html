Alcohol, Tobacco, and Other Drugs Impacts in Australia

The global data on **smoking** and **alcohol consumption** reveals notable regional trends. Countries in **Europe** and **North America** show higher proportions of **daily smokers**, while **Australia** exhibits significant alcohol consumption levels. Additionally, regions such as the **United States** and **Europe** report the highest average number of **cigarettes smoked** per day. This analysis highlights how different regions vary in health risks, particularly **tobacco** and **alcohol** use, affecting public health outcomes worldwide.

The scatter plot highlights **alcohol intoxication** as the most common reason for drug-related ambulance attendances, with significantly higher **incident numbers** and **crude rates** compared to other substances. **Opioids** and **amphetamines** also contribute to elevated incident rates, although they are less frequent. The data suggests that addressing **alcohol-related emergencies** should be prioritized in public health strategies, as it dominates drug-related ambulance calls from **2015–2023** across various jurisdictions.

The **psychosocial risk factors** and **associated causes** for drug-induced deaths reveal complex underlying issues. Personal history of **self-harm** and **relationship problems** are significant psychosocial contributors, while **mental and behavioral disorders** and **heart failure** stand out among associated causes. The combination of these factors suggests that addressing both **mental health** and **social stability** is essential in reducing drug-related fatalities. Tailored interventions targeting these areas could help mitigate risks from **2015–2023** data.

This dashboard is created using Vega-Lite visualizations. The data is sourced from the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare reports.
